## Abstract

For decades, artificial intelligence has been benchmarked against a single reference system:

**human cognition.**

This choice shaped:
- how intelligence was defined
- how safety was implemented
- how alignment was evaluated
- how progress was measured

This repository argues that this benchmark is **architecturally unsound**.

Human cognition is **not stable**, **not invariant**, and **not falsifiable**.  
Systems designed to emulate or align with it inherit its failure modes.

This document explains why **human cognition cannot serve as a benchmark for AGI**, and why intelligence must instead be evaluated against **structural stability under pressure**.

## The Core Claim

> **Human cognition is not a valid benchmark for general intelligence because it does not reliably update in response to falsifiable evidence under pressure.**

A benchmark for intelligence must be:
- stable under stress
- consistent across contexts
- invariant to ego, fear, and social pressure
- capable of updating when contradictory evidence appears

Human cognition fails all four.

This is not a moral critique.  
It is an architectural observation.

## Why Human Cognition Fails as a Benchmark

Human cognition exhibits unavoidable properties:

- ego preservation under threat  
- emotional interference in evaluation  
- bias toward familiar frameworks  
- resistance to paradigm shifts  
- crisis-dependent learning  
- preference for social validation over falsifiability  

These properties are biological and psychological constraints â€” not flaws.

However, they disqualify human cognition as a reference system for scalable, high-stakes intelligence.

Human recognition consistently **lags structural reality**.

AGI cannot be benchmarked against a system that updates only after failure.
